# Champion/Challenger pitfalls — the ways a challenger looks better but isn't

Every entry is a **false-promotion trap**: a challenger that clears a naive gate while being equal or worse in reality. Grouped by domain, then **cross-cutting**. Each has: the trap, why it fools you, the guard. Several entries close with a **field scar** (*◆*) — a paraphrased real incident — to show the trap is not hypothetical.

> The unifying lesson: **a single number going up is never sufficient evidence to promote.** Almost every trap below is a way for that number to go up without the underlying thing improving.

## ML model deployment

- **Train/test leakage (data contamination).** The challenger was tuned on data that also appears in its "held-out" eval → the held-out score is memorization, not generalization. The dominant form in sequential/recommender settings is **temporal leakage** — splitting without respecting a *global timeline* lets the model learn from interactions unavailable at prediction time (the exact analog of look-ahead bias). Critically, leakage distorts not just absolute scores but the **relative ranking of models** — a leaky benchmark can rank the truly-*worse* challenger higher (Ji et al., ACM TOIS 2023, across BPR/NeuMF/SASRec/LightGCN). **Guard:** enforce a *time-respecting, disjoint* split, key both sides identically, and **fail loud** if any train item is found in the eval set — don't silently score it. *◆ Field scar: a model scored ~0.85 "in-sample" but ~0.63 on a supposedly held-out set; the keys differed by a normalization step, so a chunk of the trained examples silently leaked into "held-out" until both sides were keyed identically.*
- **Vacuous challenger (no-op change that "passes").** A challenger that is functionally identical to the champion will tie on every metric and clear a margin-or-tie gate while changing nothing. **Guard:** assert the challenger is *materially different* before trusting a non-loss (e.g. its output distribution differs from the base; the diff is non-empty). A tie can mean "as good" or "didn't actually run." *◆ Field scar: a batch of model adapters passed eval at a 100% tie rate — because a training bug left the adapter weights at exactly zero, so they were the champion wearing a different name.*
- **Offline–online gap.** Offline metrics (held-out accuracy, NLL) improve but the live metric doesn't, because the offline set doesn't reflect live distribution or the serving path. **Guard:** treat offline as a *filter*, not the gate; confirm online with shadow then canary. Name the offline instrument's blind spot.
- **Distribution shift / stale held-out.** The held-out set ages; the world moves; a challenger that wins on last quarter's data loses on this quarter's. **Guard:** refresh held-out from recent traffic; prefer time-forward (walk-forward) splits over random ones.
- **Self-judge inflation.** Using the model (or its family) to grade its own outputs measures consistency, not quality, and rewards stylistic agreement. **Guard:** a *disagreeing* judge, blind pairwise comparison, or a judge-free reference metric (likelihood against a held reference). *◆ Field scar: a challenger whose outputs "looked sharper" by a self-similarity score lost outright once a blind pairwise judge compared it to the champion — the score measured confidence, not correctness.*
- **Held-out is necessary but NOT sufficient.** A frozen test set / paper-trading window does **not** by itself close the gap — it ignores *how many candidates were tried before selection* and breaks down when the metric has memory. Worse, under a mean-reverting/compensation process, *more* in-sample optimization produces *worse* out-of-sample results (a strongly negative IS↔OOS relation even on a trendless series — Bailey & López de Prado, AMS Notices 2014). **Guard:** combine held-out with a trial-count correction (next bullet) and a walk-forward split; never treat "passed the held-out" as the whole gate.
- **Bandit chasing noise.** Adaptive allocation (multi-armed bandit) shifts traffic to an arm that's ahead *early by chance*, starving the true winner and locking in noise — and breaks clean causal inference. **Guard:** use fixed A/B splits when you need a trustworthy *decision*; reserve bandits for pure regret-minimization where a clean verdict isn't the goal.

## Online controlled experiments (web A/B)

- **Peeking / p-hacking.** Watching a live test and stopping the moment it crosses significance massively inflates false positives — a fixed-horizon p-value is only valid if you look *once*, at the planned n. ~10 looks raises the false-positive rate from 5% to **>40%** (Johari 2022; Kohavi 2022). **Guard:** pre-set the sample size from the MDE and run to the pre-determined duration, OR use a **sequential test** (always-valid p-values / group-sequential / mSPRT, as Optimizely deploys) explicitly designed for anytime stopping. Caveat: sequential methods aren't well-suited to monitoring many metrics (OEC + guardrails) at once.
- **Underpowered test → promoting noise.** Too little sample → can't distinguish the real effect from zero; a "win" is within noise. **Guard:** compute required n from the minimum detectable effect *before* launch; a sample-size floor is a hard gate conjunct. **CUPED** (pre-experiment covariate) cuts variance by ~50% on real systems (Bing: 45/52/49%), reaching a trustworthy verdict in ~half the sample — *but only* if the covariate is **unaffected by the treatment** (else it adjusts away part of the real effect; see the Goodhart cross-cutting trap).
- **Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM).** The actual split deviates from intended (e.g. 50/50 → 48/52) beyond chance → randomization or logging is broken; every downstream number is suspect even if it looks great. An undiagnosed SRM can **completely flip the apparent direction** of a result (ExP's MSN-carousel case: bot-filtering removed the most-engaged users from one arm, turning a true positive negative until SRM was found). **Guard:** an automated **chi-square** SRM check (not eyeballing the ratio) at a conservative threshold (**Microsoft ExP gates at p<0.0005**, *before* revealing results) that *invalidates* the experiment on failure, plus A/A tests to establish baseline trust.
- **Novelty / primacy effect.** Users react to *change itself* — a new UI spikes engagement then regresses. A short test promotes a transient. **Guard:** run past the decay; inspect the trend, not just the average; segment new vs returning users.
- **Simpson's paradox.** The challenger wins in aggregate but loses in every segment (or vice-versa) due to mix shift. **Guard:** pre-declare key segments and check the effect holds within them, not just on the pooled average.
- **Network / interference effects.** Treatment leaks between units (social graphs, shared marketplaces, shared inventory) so the control is contaminated and the measured lift is wrong. **Guard:** cluster-randomize (by region/community) when units interact; don't trust user-level splits in networked products.
- **Guardrail blindness.** Optimizing the goal metric while a guardrail silently degrades (latency, errors, unsubscribes, refusal rate). Microsoft ExP treats guardrails as a *distinct mandatory class* — "aspects of the product we don't want to degrade but won't necessarily improve" — split into organizational (page-load, crash/error rate) and trust-related (SRM itself). **Guard:** pre-declare guardrail metrics, *alert on them during the run*, and have a regression **block promotion or trigger rollback regardless of the headline metric**. Maps directly to ML guardrails (latency/cost/fairness) and trading risk limits (drawdown/turnover).
- **Multiple metrics / segments fishing.** Test enough metrics or slices and one will look significant by luck. **Guard:** designate one primary metric up front; correct for multiplicity on the rest; treat secondaries as hypotheses, not wins.

## Quant trading / backtesting

- **Look-ahead bias.** The backtest uses information not available at decision time (a price, a revision, a label fixed after the fact) → fantastic backtest, dead live. **Guard:** point-in-time data; capture the archive **at ingest time, eagerly** — a frozen record of what the system actually knew at the decision, never reconstructed post-hoc with hindsight; freeze inputs at the decision timestamp; the freeze-replay test.
- **Survivorship bias.** Backtesting only on instruments that still exist drops the failures → inflated returns. **Guard:** include delisted/dead names in the universe.
- **Backtest overfitting / selection bias (the best-of-N trap).** Picking the best of many strategies/parameter sets guarantees an impressive backtest that's mostly luck: the expected *maximum* in-sample Sharpe across N candidates grows with N **even at zero true skill** — ≈ √(2 ln N), so just **N=10 trials yields an expected best Sharpe of 1.57 on pure noise** (Bailey & López de Prado). There's a **Minimum Backtest Length**: with 5 years of data, >~45 independent configs almost guarantees IS-Sharpe≈1 / OOS-Sharpe≈0; with 2 years the limit is ~7. **Guard:** (1) **log and report N** — the number of candidates tried — or the gate is *non-falsifiable* ("when a backtest does not report the configurations computed, it is impossible to assess overfitting"); (2) deflate the threshold for N and non-normality (**Deflated Sharpe Ratio**: promote only if P[true Sharpe > best-of-N noise benchmark SR₀] clears confidence); (3) walk-forward / out-of-sample; (4) prefer fewer, theory-driven candidates. *Note: correlated candidates (shared base, overlapping hyperparameters) inflate "independent N" — the penalty should use an effective N, not the raw count.*
- **Paper-vs-live slippage.** Paper/backtest fills assume prices and liquidity the real market won't give; costs, slippage, and market impact erase the edge. A *capped-live* run on the real venue exists precisely because a backtest can't see real fills — and a paper-trading sandbox often has different mechanics from the production broker, so it isn't a substitute either. **Guard:** a real-fill instrument (capped-live) separate from the edge instrument (backtest); model costs conservatively.
- **Regime change.** The strategy fit a market regime that has ended; the backtest window doesn't contain the tail that kills it. **Guard:** test across regimes; don't let a quiet window certify tail-risk behavior; the dwell can't manufacture a once-in-decade event, so don't claim edge from a calm lease — claim it from backtest across history.

## Cross-cutting (apply to all three)

- **Multiple comparisons / best-of-N — the single most universal trap.** This is the same failure in all three domains: a challenger that is *the max of many noisy trials* looks good purely because you tried many. Apparent performance grows mechanically with the number of candidates (≈√(2 ln N) at zero skill) — true of strategy sweeps, ML hyperparameter/hat sweeps, AND web tests that watch many metrics/segments. A gate that **ignores how many challengers were tried will systematically auto-promote noise.** **Guard:** count and *record* N (trials, metrics, segments, hat variants); raise the bar with N (deflated-Sharpe / Bonferroni-or-FDR style correction); designate one primary metric up front; treat the held-out as necessary-not-sufficient. *◆ Field scar: a pool that spawns many candidate models and promotes whichever scores best on held-out is exactly this trap — a per-candidate sample-size floor bounds the noise of any single candidate, but the **selection across many candidates** still needs an N-aware bar on top.*
- **Goodhart's law — gating on the metric you optimize.** Once the gate metric *is* the optimization target, the challenger finds ways to move it without improving the real thing. A concrete, mechanism-level instance: **CUPED is unbiased only if its covariate is unaffected by the treatment** — use a covariate the change actually moves and you "adjust away" part of the true effect (Deng/Kohavi, WSDM 2013). Same shape as a model grading its own output (proves consistency, not quality) or optimizing on the very backtest you then gate on. **Guard:** hold-out judges, guardrail metrics the optimizer doesn't see, **enum collapse** so a fractional gain can't tip an automated decision, and a disagreeing evaluator; never let the gate metric be coupled to the change under test.
- **Moving the bar after seeing results.** Loosening the threshold, extending the window for a favorite, or redefining "win" once the numbers are in. **Guard:** **pre-registration** — fix metrics, thresholds, sample size and exit plan before the run, in an immutable record (a commit trailer / contract). Constants are **immutable-downward**: extend, never shorten.
- **Single-score gate.** One metric crossing one threshold is gameable and noisy. **Guard:** an **AND of independent conjuncts** (effect ∧ sample/time floor ∧ no-guardrail-trip ∧ clean-reasoning); each covers a different failure mode.
- **Conflating instruments.** Using one instrument to answer a question it's structurally blind to (a backtest for mechanics, a shadow run for slippage, an offline score for live shift). **Guard:** keep instruments disjoint; for each, *write down its blind spot* and assign a second instrument to cover it.
- **Promoting on luck / ignoring reasoning.** A challenger that won *for the wrong reason* (or by chance) is more dangerous than one that simply lost — it'll fail unpredictably later. **Guard:** make clean reasoning a *hard gate, not a tiebreaker*; don't promote a result you can't explain. (A `DISQUALIFY` verdict should kill the candidate outright, not merely discount one data point.)
- **Symmetric (slow) rollback.** If reverting is as gated and slow as promoting, a bad champion lingers. **Guard:** the **asymmetry principle** — promotion gated/slow/maybe-human; reversion unconditional/instant/automatic; the kill switch never waits on the dwell.
- **No watch after promote.** Treating promotion as the finish line; live behavior drifts unobserved. **Guard:** every promotion enters a *watch* state with pre-declared revert triggers until confirmed-operational (and confirmed-positive for improvement claims).
- **Auto-promoting a high-blast-radius change.** Automating the promote of something whose worst case is irreversible. **Guard:** scale the evidence bar and the human-sign-off requirement to **blast radius** (reversibility × compounding), not diff size. Toward-known-good can auto; toward-irreversible-risk may need a human.
- **Gating data as if it were system (or vice-versa).** Routing routine data updates through the heavy promotion gate (too slow) or letting a code/prompt change reach the consequence ungated (unsafe). **Guard:** the **freeze-replay test** to draw the system/data line; gate system, CRUD data with runtime guards.

## The one-paragraph version

A challenger looks better but isn't whenever the comparison is **contaminated** (leakage, look-ahead, survivorship), **noisy** (underpowered, peeking, multiple comparisons), **transient** (novelty, regime, distribution shift), **broken** (SRM, network effects, vacuous change), **self-graded** (Goodhart, self-judge), or **measured by the wrong instrument** (backtest for mechanics, offline for live). The defenses compose into one shape: **pre-register an AND-gate of disjoint conjuncts measured by disjoint instruments whose blind spots you've named, collapse the decision to an enum, keep rollback instant and asymmetric, and watch after you promote.**

## Sources

Primary/peer-reviewed authorities backing the quantitative claims above (deep-research, 24/25 claims confirmed 3-0 on adversarial verification):

- **Bailey & López de Prado**, *The Probability of Backtest Overfitting* / *Pseudo-Mathematics and Financial Charlatanism* (AMS Notices, 2014; SSRN 2308659/2460551) — best-of-N (√(2 ln N), N=10⇒1.57), Minimum Backtest Length, Deflated Sharpe Ratio, "report N or the gate is non-falsifiable," IS↔OOS negative relation.
- **Microsoft ExP / Kohavi** — *Patterns of Trustworthy Experimentation* (ExP), *Diagnosing SRM in A/B Testing*, *Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments* (Kohavi/Tang/Xu, Ch. 1) — SRM chi-square at p<0.0005, guardrail-metric doctrine, peeking (10 looks ⇒ FPR>40%), run-to-duration.
- **Larsen et al.**, *Statistical Challenges in Online Controlled Experiments* (The American Statistician, 2023) — peeking, sequential/always-valid p-values (mSPRT), SRM.
- **Deng, Xu, Kohavi, Walker**, *CUPED* (WSDM 2013) — ρ² variance reduction (~50% on Bing) and the covariate-must-be-treatment-independent (Goodhart) condition.
- **Ji et al.**, *A Critical Study on Data Leakage in Recommender System Offline Evaluation* (ACM TOIS, 2023; arXiv 2010.11060) — temporal leakage flips model *rankings*, not just absolute scores.

*Refuted and excluded:* the claim that SRM's missing users are *generally* the treatment-most-impacted (survivorship) — SRM invalidates results, but that specific mechanism wasn't supported as a general rule.

*Open (not closed by this research, flagged for the gate designer):* the exact auto-promote-vs-human threshold; how to estimate effective N for *correlated* challengers; safe rollback mechanics (shadow→canary→ramp) — see Netflix/Google **Kayenta** automated canary analysis; and novelty/network/Simpson's effects (named but not independently verified here — see Kohavi's long-term-effects chapter).
